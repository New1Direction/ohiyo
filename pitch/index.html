The problem

Your conversations aren't yours.

Group chat became where we actually live online — and we handed all of it to platforms that read it, sell around it, and lock the good parts behind a paywall.

  • ✕
    Plaintext on someone else's serversDiscord stores your DMs and every server message in the clear. The company — and anyone who compels it — can read them.
  • ✕
    The good stuff is rentedBigger uploads, custom emoji, HD video, themes, and community perks are split across Nitro and Boosts. You pay monthly to decorate a place you don't own.
  • ✕
    You can't host it or trust itClosed source, closed servers. No self-hosting, no audit, no exit. Your community is a guest.
The idea

Take the parts people love about Discord — and give communities privacy, portability, and ownership.

End-to-end encrypted DMs and group chats built on the Signal Protocol, wrapped in a full community platform: servers, voice, video, screen-share, roles, polls, events, rich embeds, and one-tap managed servers you can export or self-host.

The product line

Six pillars, one home.

Instant Servers

Your own server, in one tap.

We host it so it just works — and the control plane only manages infrastructure metadata. Owners can create, sleep, wake, export, graduate, or delete an instance from the app. When you leave, the raw Server Pack carries the SQLite snapshot, uploads, manifest, and checksum proof.

Under the hood

No hand-rolled crypto. The real thing.

HandshakeX3DH

Asynchronous key agreement — message someone who's offline, forward-secret from the first byte.

1:1 sessionsDouble Ratchet

Per-message keys with forward secrecy & post-compromise recovery, via the audited libsignal.

GroupsSender keys + epochs

Every membership change bumps an epoch and rekeys; a removed member can't read a single future message.

TrustSafety numbers

Out-of-band verification across all of a contact's devices; a swapped key raises a loud banner.

At restVault + backup

Desktop seals keys in an AES-256-GCM vault; recovery is a self-custody code (PBKDF2 → AES-GCM).

For E2E DMs and group chats, the server relays ciphertext: sealed envelopes between devices, membership enforcement, and no readable message body. Rust integration tests prove a removed member loses future access and a pinned identity can't be silently swapped.
